## 2. Outdoor Benchmarks in Detail

### 2.1 ICASSP 2023 Outdoor Pathloss Challenge

**Dataset:** RadioMap3DSeer — 256×256 synthetic radio maps over 6 procedurally
generated cities (Ankara, Berlin, Glasgow, Ljubljana, London, Tel Aviv).
Test on Istanbul — also procedurally generated, different seed.
Tx is fixed to rooftop (~6–20 m). Normalized RMSE reported over the linearly
scaled image domain of the rooftop 3D dataset. Building interior pixels masked to 0.

| Rank | Method | Normalized RMSE | Physical RMSE (exact) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | PMNet | 0.0383 | **1.1107 dB** |
| 2 | Agile / REM-U-Net | 0.0451 | 1.3079 dB |
| 3 | PPNet | 0.0507 | 1.4703 dB |
| — | REM-Net+ (late, 2024) | 0.0349 | **1.0121 dB** |
| — | RMTransformer (2025) | 0.0270 | **0.7830 dB** |
| — | Gao et al. (2026) | ~0.030 | **0.8700 dB** (estimated, because the source normalized RMSE is itself estimated) |

**Links:**
- Challenge results: https://radiomapchallenge.github.io/results.html
- PMNet: https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.10527
- REM-Net+: https://www.authorea.com/doi/full/10.36227/techrxiv.172226872.27268975
- RMTransformer: https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.05190
- Gao et al.: https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.08436

**Why NOT comparable to Try 66:**

1. **Same-distribution test.** Istanbul is generated by the same procedural simulator
   as training cities — the model implicitly learns the generator's patterns.
   Our city-holdout still uses simulator-generated labels, but over OSM-derived
   city geometries from 66 different cities with genuinely different morphology.
2. **Fixed rooftop Tx.** No height generalization required. Our UAV spans 12–478 m
   with continuous conditioning via sinusoidal FiLM.
3. **Building pixels masked.** Interior pixels are set to 0 error, artificially
   reducing reported RMSE by an estimated 15–20%.
4. **The challenge uses a 29 dB linear scaling window.** For the rooftop 3D
   dataset used in RadioMap3DSeer, the dataset paper reports
   `PL_thr = -104 dB` and `PL_max = -75 dB`, so the normalized image domain
   corresponds to a 29 dB interval. Our range is 0–180 dB (65–130 dB actually
   used), substantially wider and physically different.

For the methods above with published normalized RMSE, the physical value is computed
from the actual dataset mapping, not from a guessed multiplier. The dataset paper
states that the rooftop dataset is created by truncating below a threshold and
linearly scaling the remaining range to the image domain. For RadioMap3DSeer:

```text
PL_thr = -104 dB
PL_max = -75 dB
I = (PL - PL_thr) / (PL_max - PL_thr) = (PL + 104) / 29
PL = 29 * I - 104
```

Since RMSE depends on differences and the additive offset cancels out:

```text
RMSE_dB = 29 * RMSE_norm
```

Only Gao et al. remains estimated because the paper reports an improvement range
rather than a single exact normalized RMSE in the source we cite.

### Are these benchmark errors also about NLoS?

Yes, NLoS is still one of the hard parts of the ICASSP 2023 benchmark. The
important nuance is that its contribution to the reported RMSE is **compressed**
relative to our problem.

Why the reported values are so narrow:

1. **The dataset is linearly compressed into a 29 dB window.** Deep-loss pixels
   below the truncation threshold are not allowed to keep spreading arbitrarily
   far into a large tail.
2. **The Tx is always rooftop-mounted.** This reduces the amount of extreme deep
   shadowing compared with low-altitude UAV cases, where whole street corridors
   can sit in harsh NLoS.
3. **Building pixels are masked to zero error.** Some of the hardest geometrically
   blocked pixels are explicitly removed from the metric.
4. **The city generator is shared between train and test.** Even when the test city
   is unseen, the morphology family is not.

So the answer is: **yes, they still have NLoS difficulty, but the benchmark makes
that difficulty much less extreme than in our setting.**

### Why the challenge values are physically narrow

The narrow dB values do not mean the methods solve propagation perfectly. They are
narrow because the benchmark itself is narrow:

- only a 29 dB image-domain window is evaluated
- fixed rooftop deployment avoids the worst low-altitude blockage patterns
- masked building pixels reduce error on impossible / irrelevant regions
- same-generator training and test strongly reduce distribution shift

That is why a value near 1 dB on that challenge should not be interpreted as
``all NLoS problems are basically solved''.

### Quantization: uint8 is also part of the story

There is also an important label-precision issue.

For the RadioMap3DSeer challenge, the 29 dB range is stored in `uint8`, so one
gray-level step corresponds to:

```text
29 / 255 = 0.1137 dB per level
```

This is quite fine, so quantization is only a minor issue there.

For **our** dataset, however, the ground truth is effectively stored at about
**1 dB precision per level**. That is much coarser and does matter:

- irreducible quantization-noise floor:
  ```text
  sigma_q = Delta / sqrt(12) = 1 / sqrt(12) = 0.2887 dB
  ```
- sharp LoS/NLoS transitions get snapped to integer bins
- very small improvements can be hidden by label discretization

This does **not** explain a 9 dB RMSE by itself, but it does impose a real ceiling
on the final accuracy and makes sub-dB refinements harder to learn and to measure.

### 2.2 AIRMap — Generalization to Unseen Urban/Rural Environments

**Paper:** arXiv:2511.05522 (November 2025)

**Method:** Single-input U-Net autoencoder using only 2D elevation maps (terrain +
building heights). No topology class, no explicit NLoS handling, no height conditioning.

**Training:** 1.2M samples, Boston metropolitan area only.
**Test:** 4 distinct unseen environments (urban + rural, different building density).
**Result:** < **4 dB RMSE** on path gain in unseen environments. Inference in 4 ms
on NVIDIA L40S. Calibrated with 20% of field measurements → ~5% median error.

**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.05522

**Why comparable to Try 66:**
- Tests on genuinely unseen environments with different morphology
- No fine-tuning on the test environment (zero-shot generalization)

**Why different from Try 66:**
- Terrain + elevation only as input (no los_mask, no corridor weighting, no depth maps)
- Fixed terrestrial Tx height, not UAV
- Reported as path gain (not absolute path loss) — measurement calibration applied
- Boston-only training is less diverse than our 66-city dataset

**Assessment:** AIRMap's < 4 dB on calibrated path gain is excellent but
achieved on a calibration-corrected task. Their zero-shot result (before calibration)
is not explicitly reported. Our 9.41 dB is a true zero-shot, no-calibration result.

### 2.3 Tarhouni et al. — Out-of-Domain Suburban (KAUST → Rio)

**Paper:** arXiv:2510.00696 (October 2025)

**Method:** CNN-based path loss prediction, trained on KAUST suburban campus maps,
tested on a genuinely unseen city (Rio de Janeiro).
Dataset: ~2,000 samples, 64×64 maps, fixed terrestrial Tx, 2.4 GHz.

**Results:**
- In-domain (KAUST test set): ~7–9 dB RMSE
- Out-of-domain (Rio de Janeiro): **9.3–11.3 dB RMSE**

**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.00696

**Why most comparable to Try 66:**
- Only paper found that explicitly reports cross-domain city generalization
- Real city environment, not procedural generator

**Why still easier than Try 66:**
- 64×64 resolution (vs our 513×513): NLoS pixel structure is blurred away
- Single suburban campus type (KAUST) → simple morphology
- Fixed Tx height, no continuous height conditioning
- Only 2 cities total (KAUST train → Rio test), not 66-city holdout

**Assessment:** Our 9.41 dB at 513×513 with 66-city holdout and continuous UAV
height is directly competitive with their 9.3–11.3 dB at 64×64 with single
environment transfer. This is the most honest like-for-like comparison.

### 2.4 PathFinder — Multi-Transmitter Distribution Shift

**Paper:** arXiv:2512.14150 (December 2025)

**Method:** Disentangled Feature Encoding + Mask-Guided Low-Rank Attention.
Addresses distribution shift when models trained on single-Tx environments
are tested on multi-Tx configurations. New benchmark: S2MT-RPP.

**Results:** Significantly outperforms PMNet and PPNet on multi-Tx generalization.
Exact dB values not published in preprint (qualitative improvement demonstrated
via map visualizations showing predicted path loss boundaries).

**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.14150

**Why relevant:** Validates the difficulty of cross-configuration generalization —
their best models still show boundary artifacts in unseen multi-Tx setups.

---

### 2.5 WiCo-PG — UAV-to-Ground Foundation Model

**Paper:** arXiv:2511.15030 (November 2025)

**Method:** Dual VQGANs + Transformer + Frequency-guided Mixture of Experts.
Trained on a new multi-modal dataset with diverse UAV altitudes and frequencies.
Uses RGB images (drone camera) as auxiliary input for cross-modal conditioning.

**Results:** NMSE = 0.012. Outperforms LLM-based baseline by > 6.98 dB.
Few-shot (2.7% of samples): outperforms LLM4PG by > 1.37 dB.

**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.15030

**Why relevant to Try 66:**
- One of the only papers that explicitly addresses variable UAV altitude in path
  loss prediction (our primary novelty)
- Uses Mixture of Experts for multi-frequency, related to our sinusoidal FiLM
  for continuous height

**Why hard to compare directly:**
- NMSE metric (not dB RMSE) — conversion requires knowing the variance of their
  path loss values
- Proprietary dataset, not publicly available
- RGB images as input (not available in our dataset)

---

### 2.6 Gao et al. — Multi-Layer Segmentation with Weighting Map

**Paper:** arXiv:2601.08436 (January 2026)

**Method:** ResNet-based deep learning with:
- Tx/Rx depth maps (distance to nearest obstacle from transmitter/receiver)
- Propagation corridor weighting map (emphasizes pixels near direct Tx-Rx path)
- Multi-layer segmentation for building-type classification

**Results:**
- 1.2–3.0 dB improvement over PPNet, RPNet, and ViT baselines
- 60% FLOPs reduction vs baseline models
- Tested on ICASSP 2023 and ITU Challenge 2024 datasets (same-distribution)

**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.08436

**Why directly relevant to Try 66:**
This paper motivated Try 66's inclusion of `tx_depth_map` and
`corridor_weighting` loss. Both are already implemented in our pipeline.
Our results confirm Gao's finding that these features provide meaningful gains.

## 4. What Makes Try 66 Unique in the Literature

No published paper as of April 2026 simultaneously addresses all of:

| Feature | Try 66 | Most SOTA papers |
|---|---|---|
| Outdoor (not indoor) | ✅ | ✅ |
| 513×513 pixel maps | ✅ | ❌ (256×256 or smaller) |
| Continuous UAV Tx height (12–478 m) | ✅ | ❌ (fixed terrestrial Tx) |
| City-level holdout (unseen city geometries) | ✅ | ❌ (same-generator or fine-tuned) |
| 66-city training diversity | ✅ | ❌ (1–10 cities typical) |
| No calibration at test time | ✅ | Sometimes ❌ |
| Topology-partitioned experts | ✅ | ❌ |

The sinusoidal FiLM conditioning on Tx height is — to the best of our search —
not published elsewhere for this specific task. WiCo-PG uses per-altitude datasets
(not continuous conditioning). RadioLAM uses discrete height bins. Our approach
treats height as a continuous parameter and conditions every encoder layer on it.

### 4.1 Why continuous UAV height is a separate problem

Continuous UAV height conditioning is not a minor metadata change. It changes the
entire propagation regime:

- at low altitude, buildings occlude aggressively and NLoS corridors dominate
- at medium altitude, diffraction and partial rooftop clearance interact
- at high altitude, the propagation becomes increasingly LoS-like and smoother

So the model is not just learning one radio-map distribution. It is learning a
**family of distributions indexed by altitude**. A fixed-height benchmark only asks
the model to solve one slice of that family. Try 66 must interpolate across the
full 12–478 m range.

This is one of the strongest novelty claims of the project and should be stated
explicitly in any supervisor-facing summary.

**Note on Try 64:** The 7.76 dB result was at 128×128. At that resolution, hard NLoS
pixels (building edge diffraction, narrow alleys) are spatially averaged in the
ground truth, making the task artificially easier. Not comparable to 513×513 results.

### 5.1 Why the remaining gap is mostly NLoS

The last 4–5 dB are not missing because the model is uniformly weak everywhere.
They are missing because the residual error is concentrated in a small subset of
structurally hard pixels:

- LoS regions are already relatively well modelled
- the free-space-based prior is helpful in open zones
- hard error lives near building corners, canyon transitions, and deep shadow
  regions where diffraction dominates

This is why 5 dB is both plausible and difficult: the model does not need to
improve everywhere, but it does need to solve the most physics-sensitive cases.

### 5.2 Canonical failure modes

For the thesis, these are the best three concrete failure modes to mention:

| Failure mode | Typical symptom | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Street canyon shadowing | Prediction too optimistic along long blocked corridors | Missing explicit multi-edge diffraction prior |
| Building-corner transition | Abrupt edge error when going from LoS to NLoS | Boundary handling is under-modelled |
| Deep block shadow | Underestimation behind dense highrise clusters | Prior mismatch dominates residual learning |

These examples are much stronger than describing the issue as generic “high NLoS RMSE”.

| Benchmark | Our result | Assessment |
|---|---|---|
| ICASSP 2023 outdoor (same-dist.) | 9.41 vs 0.7830–1.1107 dB | Different problem, not fair |
| AIRMap (calibrated unseen outdoor) | 9.41 vs < 4 dB | Different inputs (calibration applied) |
| **Tarhouni (unseen city, 64×64)** | **9.41 vs 9.3–11.3 dB** | **Comparable, we are competitive** |

**Short answer: Try 66 is state-of-the-art for its specific problem formulation.**
No published paper simultaneously achieves < 10 dB RMSE on:
- outdoor environments
- 512+ pixel maps
- continuous UAV height conditioning
- city-level holdout over unseen city geometries
